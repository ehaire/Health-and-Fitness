For each strength session, pick 2 or 3 exercises from each muscle group below. You don't need to do every exercise every time — mix and match based on how you feel, and switch it up each session to keep things from getting stale.
Legs
Legs Squat 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointed slightly out. Have a chair or wall within arm's reach for balance if needed. Push your hips back and bend your knees, lowering as far as comfortable while keeping your chest up and knees tracking over your toes. Push through your heels to stand back up. Go only as low as feels controlled — depth will improve over time.
Progression Once squats feel comfortable and controlled, hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides to add resistance.
Legs Sumo Squat 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand with feet wider than shoulder-width, toes turned out at about 45°. Sit straight down between your knees, keeping your chest tall and your knees pushing out in line with your toes. Push through your heels to stand. The wider stance takes pressure off the knees and works the inner thighs more than a regular squat.
Progression Once comfortable, hold one dumbbell with both hands hanging between your legs, or hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides.
Legs Reverse Lunge 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Stand tall, feet together, one hand on a wall or chair back for balance if needed. Step one foot back and lower your back knee toward the floor, keeping your front knee directly over your ankle. Push through your front heel to step back to standing. Alternate legs each rep.
Progression Once lunges feel controlled, hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides to add resistance.
Legs Step-Up 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Stand in front of a sturdy step or the bottom stair. Place one foot fully on the step and drive through that heel to lift yourself up until that leg is straight. Bring the other foot up to meet it, then step back down one foot at a time. Lead with the same leg for all reps on one side, then switch. Hold a wall or railing for balance as needed.
Progression Once step-ups feel solid, hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides to increase the challenge.
Legs Romanian Deadlift (RDL) 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand with feet hip-width apart, a very slight bend in your knees — roughly 10 to 15 degrees, just enough so they're not locked out. That knee angle stays fixed throughout the movement; this is not a squat. Push your hips straight back as if you're trying to touch the wall behind you, and let your torso hinge forward slowly as your hips travel back. Lower only as far as your hips will allow — the moment your hips stop moving backward, that is your end point. Do not continue hinging lower if your hips can't go with it, as that shifts the load into your lower back rather than your hamstrings. Drive your hips forward to return to standing. You should feel a deep stretch through the back of your legs at the bottom.
Progression Once comfortable with bodyweight, hold a dumbbell in each hand in front of your thighs, letting them travel down your legs as you hinge.
What this works Primarily the hamstrings and glutes, with the lower back stabilizing. One of the most effective posterior chain exercises you can do.
Glutes & Hips
Glutes Hip Thrust 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Sit on the floor with your upper back resting against the couch cushion, knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Drive through your heels and squeeze your glutes to lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees. Hold for a second at the top, then lower back down slowly.
Progression Place a dumbbell, a heavy book, or any weighted object across your hips to add resistance. Hold it in place with both hands so it doesn't shift during the movement.
Glutes Donkey Kick 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Floor version — if feeling good Get on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips. Keeping your knee bent at 90°, kick one leg back and up until your foot points toward the ceiling and your thigh is parallel to the floor. Squeeze your glute at the top, then lower your leg slowly and with control — resist the urge to let it drop back down. The slow return is where a significant portion of the work happens. Complete all reps on one side before switching.
Standing version — on tougher days Stand facing a wall or chair back for support. Kick one leg straight back, squeezing your glute as your leg lifts behind you. Keep your upper body still and avoid arching your lower back. Then lower your leg back slowly and with control rather than letting it swing back into place. Letting the leg drop eliminates most of the benefit of the exercise. Complete all reps on one side before switching.
Back
Back Single Arm Dumbbell Row 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Place one hand and the same-side knee on a chair or couch for support. Hold a dumbbell in the opposite hand, arm hanging straight down. Pull the dumbbell up toward your hip — not your shoulder, so your hand ends up roughly at your waistline when your elbow is fully bent. Keep your elbow close to your body and your back flat throughout. Lower slowly.
Back Bent Over Row 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand with feet shoulder-width apart holding a dumbbell in each hand. Hinge forward at the hips about 45°, keeping your back flat and knees slightly bent. Let the dumbbells hang in front of you, then pull both toward your hips simultaneously, elbows close to your sides. Squeeze at the top, then lower slowly. Think of it as pulling your elbows straight back rather than lifting the weights up.
Back position If your lower back starts to round or ache, stand more upright. A slight hinge is all that's needed.
Chest
Chest Wall Push-Up 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand facing a wall, arms extended, hands flat on the wall at shoulder height and slightly wider than shoulder-width. Bend your elbows and lower your chest toward the wall, then push back to the start. The further your feet are from the wall, the harder it is.
Progression Wall push-ups → hands on a countertop → knee push-ups on the floor.
Chest Dumbbell Floor Fly 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Lie on your back on the floor with a dumbbell in each hand, arms extended straight up over your chest, palms facing each other. With a slight bend in your elbows, slowly lower both arms out to the sides until your upper arms rest on the floor or you feel a stretch across your chest. Bring them back up like you're hugging a large tree. Keep the same slight elbow bend throughout.
Weight tip Start with 5lb dumbbells. The arc of the fly puts more demand on the shoulder than it looks — lighter is right here.
Shoulders
Shoulders Seated Dumbbell Press 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Sit in a chair with back support. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at shoulder height, palms facing forward. Press both dumbbells straight up until your arms are almost fully extended, then lower back to shoulder height slowly.
Shoulders Lateral Raise 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand or sit with a dumbbell in each hand at your sides, palms facing in. With a slight bend in your elbows, raise both arms out to the sides until they reach shoulder height — no higher. Lower slowly. Keep your shoulders relaxed down throughout — avoid shrugging.
Weight tip The 2 or 5lb dumbbells are the right call here. Lateral raises feel much heavier than they look and most people go too heavy.
Shoulders Front Raise 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand with a dumbbell in each hand in front of your thighs, palms facing down. Raise one arm straight forward to shoulder height, keeping a slight bend in the elbow. Lower it back down, then raise the other arm. Alternate sides for each rep. Keep your core tight and avoid leaning back as you lift.
Biceps
Biceps Dumbbell Curl 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Stand or sit with a dumbbell in each hand, palms facing forward, arms at your sides. Curl both dumbbells up toward your shoulders by bending at the elbow, keeping your upper arms still. Lower slowly.
Biceps Hammer Curl 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Same starting position as a regular curl, but with your palms facing each other — thumbs pointing up — instead of forward. Curl both dumbbells up toward your shoulders keeping that neutral grip throughout. This variation works slightly different parts of the bicep and the forearm compared to the standard curl.
Triceps
Triceps Dumbbell Kickback 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Hinge forward slightly at the hips, bracing your free hand on a chair. Hold a dumbbell with your upper arm pinned against your side, elbow bent at 90°. Straighten your arm back and up by extending the elbow until your arm is straight, then slowly return. Keep your upper arm still the whole time.
Triceps Tricep Dip 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Sit on the edge of a sturdy chair or couch with your hands gripping the edge beside your hips, fingers pointing forward. Slide your hips off the edge. Bend your elbows and lower your hips toward the floor, keeping your back close to the chair. Push back up by straightening your arms. Keep your feet flat on the floor and knees bent to make it more manageable.
Make it easier Keep your feet closer to your body with more knee bend. The closer your feet, the less weight your arms have to handle.
Core
Core Dead Bug 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Lie on your back, arms pointing straight up toward the ceiling, knees bent at 90° in the air. Slowly lower one arm back overhead and extend the opposite leg out, keeping your lower back pressed into the floor. Bring them back and switch sides. Move slowly and controlled.
Why this over crunches No strain on the neck or spine. Works deep core stability.
Core Bird Dog 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps each ▼
Start on all fours with hands directly under shoulders and knees under hips. At the same time, extend one arm straight forward and the opposite leg straight back, keeping your hips level and your back flat. Hold for a second, then bring them back in and switch sides. Avoid letting your hips rotate or your lower back arch.
vs. Dead Bug Same concept as the dead bug but on all fours — easier to get into for some people, harder to balance.
Core Seated Knee Tuck 2-3 sets · 8-15 reps ▼
Sit on the edge of a sturdy chair, hands gripping the sides for support, leaning back slightly. Lift both knees toward your chest by contracting your core, then lower your feet back down without letting them fully touch the floor. Keep the movement slow and controlled — no swinging.
Make it easier Lift one knee at a time, alternating sides.
Cardio
Cardio Elliptical — Steady State 20–30 min ▼
Set the elliptical to a comfortable resistance and maintain a consistent pace for the full session. Your target is a heart rate of around 130 bpm — you should be breathing noticeably but still able to speak in short sentences. This is the foundation session and the one to default to when energy is lower.
Good to know Steady state cardio at moderate intensity is highly effective for fat burning and building your aerobic base without overdoing it on recovery demand.
Cardio Elliptical — Intervals 20–25 min ▼
Alternate between a hard push and an easy recovery throughout the session.
Session structure
  1. 5 min — easy warm-up pace
  2. 1 min hard — increase resistance or speed, push your effort to a 7–8 out of 10
  3. 2 min easy — drop back to a comfortable recovery pace
  4. Repeat the hard/easy cycle 5–6 times
  5. 3–5 min — easy cool-down
Why this works Intervals burn more calories in less time than steady state and keep your metabolism elevated after the session ends.
Cardio Elliptical — Resistance Ladder 25–30 min ▼
Keep your pace consistent throughout but climb resistance levels every few minutes, then come back down.
Session structure
  1. 5 min — resistance level 3 (warm-up)
  2. 5 min — resistance level 5
  3. 5 min — resistance level 7
  4. 5 min — resistance level 5
  5. 5 min — resistance level 3 (cool-down)
Adjust to your machine Resistance numbers vary by elliptical model. The goal is a noticeable step up in effort each level — adjust numbers accordingly.
Cardio Home Circuit — Low Impact 20–30 min ▼
Move through each exercise back to back with minimal rest. Rest 60–90 seconds after completing all exercises, then repeat the circuit 3–4 times total. Focus on keeping your heart rate up rather than going fast — controlled movement throughout.
Circuit — repeat 3–4 rounds
  1. March in place — 45 seconds, drive your knees up with purpose
  2. Step touches — 45 seconds, step side to side reaching your opposite arm across
  3. Stair walk — up and down one flight or step up and down on the bottom stair, 45 seconds
  4. Low impact jumping jack — 45 seconds, step one foot out at a time instead of jumping, arms still go overhead
  5. Standing bicycle — 45 seconds, hands behind head, bring one knee up while rotating the opposite elbow toward it, alternate sides
  6. Heel-to-toe walk — 30 seconds, walk forward placing heel directly in front of toe each step, then walk back
Cardio Home Circuit — Strength Cardio 20–25 min ▼
Combines light dumbbells with movement to keep the heart rate up while adding a small muscle stimulus. Move at a pace that keeps you breathing hard. Rest 60–90 seconds between rounds.
Circuit — repeat 3 rounds
  1. Squat to overhead press — 12 reps, squat down and as you stand press both dumbbells overhead (use 5–8 lbs)
  2. March in place — 45 seconds, drive your knees up with purpose
  3. Dumbbell curl to press — 10 reps, curl the dumbbells up then press them overhead in one fluid motion
  4. Step touch with lateral raise — 45 seconds, step side to side and raise arms to shoulder height on each step (use 2–5 lbs)
  5. Reverse lunge alternating — 10 reps each leg, bodyweight only, controlled pace
  6. Bent over dumbbell row — 12 reps, hinge forward, pull both dumbbells toward your hips, lower slowly (use 8–10 lbs)
  7. Romanian Deadlift (RDL) — 12 reps, stand with feet hip-width apart and a slight bend in your knees (roughly 10-15°). Push your hips straight back while hinging forward slowly. When your hips won't push back any further, stop there and return — don't continue lowering if the hips are done moving. Bodyweight only, slow and controlled each direction.
Good to know This circuit doubles as light full-body conditioning. It won't replace a dedicated strength session, but on days you want to move without committing to a full workout, this covers both bases.
General guidelines
Rest 1–2 minutes between sets. Only add weight when the last few reps of a set start to feel easy, or when you can complete 12–15 reps with good form and still have something left in the tank. Focus on controlling the weight in both directions — especially on the way back down. The return to the starting position (called the eccentric) is when the muscle is lengthening back out after it contracted, and slowing it down is one of the most effective things you can do to build strength and see results. Letting weights drop fast is wasted effort.
